A pronoun is a word that takes the place of a noun! Instead of saying "Sarah" over and over, we can say "she." Instead of repeating "the ball," we say "it." Pronouns make our sentences shorter and less repetitive! üë§

‚ú® Examples of Pronouns:

  • üë¶ For boys/men: he, him, his
  • üëß For girls/women: she, her, hers
  • üì¶ For things: it, its
  • üë• For groups: we, they, us, them, our, their
  • üôã For yourself: I, me, my, mine

Fun Tip: "Sarah loves her dog. She plays with it every day." Can you see how "she" replaces "Sarah" and "it" replaces "dog"? üêï

üéØ Types of Pronouns

üôã

Personal Pronouns

Words that refer to specific people or things. They change based on who is speaking or being spoken about.

Examples: I, me, you, he, him, she, her, it, we, us, they, them
üéÅ

Possessive Pronouns

Words that show ownership or belonging. They tell us who something belongs to.

Examples: my, mine, your, yours, his, her, hers, its, our, ours, their, theirs
üëâ

Demonstrative Pronouns

Words that point to specific things. They help us identify which one we're talking about.

Examples: this, that, these, those
‚ùì

Interrogative Pronouns

Words used to ask questions. They help us find out information about people or things.

Examples: who, whom, whose, which, what
ü™û

Reflexive Pronouns

Words that refer back to the subject. They end in -self or -selves.

Examples: myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, themselves
üåê

Indefinite Pronouns

Words that refer to non-specific people or things. They don't point to anyone in particular.

Examples: someone, anyone, everyone, nobody, something, anything, all, some, none
üîó

Relative Pronouns

Words that connect clauses or phrases to nouns or pronouns. They relate one part of the sentence to another.

Examples: who, whom, whose, which, that
